# Review 6 — Story & Narrative Direction

**Scope:** the campaign narrative as it exists — frame, floor structure, the three routes,
the designed encounters (Incineradile intro, demonic nobility/Dissolution songs, Sasha's
maze), Nikita, and the party as characters. Sources: Master Compendium §§2.8, 3.1, 3.5, 4,
5; rulebook flavor; GDD narrative layers.
**Standard applied:** honest editorial review — the same bar as the market review, not
encouragement. "Handled" below means both table-handling difficulty and digital-conversion
cost.

---

## Overall verdict

**A collection of genuinely strong fragments that is not yet a story.** The best material
here — the Nikita/Sasha thread, the Dissolution songs, the consequences-across-decades
route structure — is *better than most published campaign writing*, full stop. But the
connective tissue is thin, the production itself (the show's cast) is missing, floors 4–6
are a label rather than a design, and the campaign currently has **no designed ending**.
The strongest latent asset is a theme that already exists in the material but hasn't been
named: every route is secretly asking *"what cures corruption, and what does the cure
cost?"* — Nullrot spreads and cures a disease; demons seek a cure for their own nature; the
Loong IS a cure being hunted; the brand cures vulnerability at the price of complicity.
Name that spine and half the missing connective tissue writes itself.

---

## 1. The frame (the show, the Corporation)

**Strong:** the satire premise (colonization propaganda as reality TV) is load-bearing and
the tone voice is already there in the item copy ("The Corporation™ claims no
responsibility…"). "No respawns" + "we're not asking" sets stakes cleanly.

**Weak — the show has no faces.** For a reality-TV story, the missing cast is the
*production*: there is no named announcer, no producer, no showrunner AI, no rival
contestants before the floors-4–6 merge, and the audience is mechanically present but
narratively invisible. Dungeon Crawler Carl works because the production is *personified*
(named AI, named corporate villains, named superfans). Recommendation: create **two
recurring production characters** minimum — an announcer voice (comedy, broadcast plane)
and a producer/showrunner (menace, occasionally intervenes diegetically) — and let named
patrons emerge later via the Ascension design, which already manufactures them.

**Tonal management note:** the project runs corporate comedy ("Sup, nerds!") beside
Holocaust-adjacent grief (Nikita) and human farms. The two-information-planes decision made
for the social director is *also the tonal firewall*: comedy lives on the broadcast plane
(announcer, ads, tickers), grief lives on the diegetic plane (what contestants actually
touch). Keep the planes tonally distinct and the whiplash becomes the point — the show
laughing over something the players experienced as tragedy is the satire working.

## 2. Floor structure & the route system

**The best structural idea: time skips make consequences visible.** Floor 2 is Floor 1's
choices *seventy years later*; the chained man is still chained. That converts player
choice into archaeology — you walk through your own decisions as history. No mainstream
tactics RPG does this. Protect this idea at all costs.

**The route-exclusivity economics are honest roguelite design** (pick one per campaign;
others resolve offscreen) and they fit the Ascension/NG+ loop — replay = a different third
of the content plus a changed world. But the payoff is currently undesigned: **the capital
(F3) needs a convergence matrix** — the state of the endgame city as a function of which
route you took *and* how the unpicked routes resolved offscreen. All three routes already
converge on the same ecology (Nullrot's disease, the Loong suppressing it, demons seeking
cures) — this reads as coincidence right now; one design pass makes it the campaign's
crowning structure. **Highest-value story work item.**

**Floors 4–6 (continent merge)** are a premise, not a design — and they hold the project's
best character (Nikita). Risk: the payoff content lives in the least-designed region.
Mitigation already half-exists: Sasha's F1 vision seeds Nikita early. Add one more echo per
floor (the scarf glimpsed, a hummed bar of the song) so the thread never goes cold.

**Route difficulty labels are moral irony** — Easy carries the heaviest guilt, Medium is a
complicity test, Hard is the compassion route. If intentional (it reads intentional), make
it legible: the Corporation rates routes by *physical survival odds only* — it cannot
measure moral damage. That's a joke and a thesis in one line of set dressing.

## 3. The routes, individually

### Easy — the mask, the chained man, Nullrot. **Grade: the best route as written.**
- **Strong:** one throughline object across 170 years; a real moral rug-pull (the
  prophecy's "right answer" = chaining a man to a wall for seventy years — and F2 makes you
  *meet* your obedience, ancient and ruined); F3 grants him agency that recontextualizes
  your guilt (fight or help the thing you created). This is a complete tragic arc.
- **Weak:** F2's "a demon blocks the exit, defeat it" is generic connective tissue — the
  route's one lazy beat; the demon should relate to the mask or the man (a failed previous
  wearer?). The mask itself has no stated origin, wants, or rules. **Nullrot's
  spread-AND-cure behavior has no stated motive** — it's the route's climax and currently
  runs on vibes. Three candidate logics (pick one): the mask balances every mercy with a
  cruelty; he's testing who deserves the cure; he IS both plague and cure and can't
  separate them. 
- **Handling:** easiest route at the table (one NPC, clear beats); cheapest digitally.

### Medium — the demon girl, the queen, the human farm. **Grade: most interesting, least finished.**
- **Strong:** it's a complicity engine — at every beat the quest-compliant action is
  monstrous (kill humans to save a demon child; assassinate the demon who helps humans;
  hand a human over for sacrifice). The route tests whether players notice they're the
  villains of someone else's story. The demonic-nobility cosmology (normal demons are
  fallen, comprehensible; nobility corrupts *by existing*) is clean and original. The
  Dissolution-songs encounter (reviewed below) lives here and is the campaign's best set
  piece.
- **Weak / broken:**
  1. **The route assumes compliance.** What happens to a party that refuses to kill the
     NPCs, refuses to feed her, refuses the assassination? Every beat needs a genuine
     refusal fork with a *different cost*, or agency is an illusion.
  2. **The brand paradox — decide if it's a feature.** The brand (reward for complicity)
     grants immunity to noble presence — which means branded parties are immune to the
     Dissolution songs, the route's most powerful content. Mercy costs you safety but buys
     you the songs; complicity buys safety and *skips the route's soul*. If intentional,
     that's brilliant — say so in the design. If accidental, fix it (partial immunity: the
     brand mutes the timer but not the song?).
  3. **Continuity bug:** the F3 human-farm leader is "the surviving F1 NPC" — a mortal
     human, ~170 years later. He needs a longevity mechanism (descendant carrying the
     grudge? demon-touched? the girl-queen keeps him alive as a pet cruelty?). The symmetry
     (the man who tried to burn a demon child now farms humans) is too good to lose to a
     timeline error.
  4. "Faction points" are referenced and undefined anywhere.
- **Handling:** hardest at the table (register control, refusal forks); digitally the
  songs encounter needs the tag-profiling analogue (below) and the licensed songs replaced.

### Hard — the Loong. **Grade: the sweetest arc, the weakest quest design.**
- **Strong:** a guardian who must be *persuaded* that everyone it protects is already gone
  is a beautiful fantasy beat (Shadow of the Colossus grief). A social boss as a route
  climax shows off the system's Charm/requirements design like nothing else. The Loong as a
  continuity character across all three floors gives the route emotional compound interest,
  and its F3 role (hunted as a cure) locks into the convergence theme.
- **Weak:** structurally it's **an escort quest three times**, and escort is the most
  hated quest shape in games. Digital mitigation: make the Loong *terrain* — it's huge, so
  fights happen ON and AROUND it (defend segments, use its body as cover/elevation), which
  converts escort into a moving-arena mechanic the hex system can own. "It finds purpose in
  a village" is a placeholder sentence where the route's midpoint emotion should be.
- **Handling:** moderate at the table; digitally the most expensive route (large-creature
  arena tech), but that tech doubles as boss infrastructure.

## 4. The designed encounters

### The Dissolution songs (demonic noble, Medium F2) — **the campaign's crown jewel.**
Personalized psychological horror, mechanically real (Mind death-timer + the feeding-window
escape), with permanent consequences that keep generating play (embrace = ghoul that
persists in story; escape = one emotion amplified near demons — a scar that's also a
roleplay prompt). The insight that Dissolution amplifies *an* emotion, **not necessarily
yours** (Sasha hears Nikita's fear) elevates the whole mechanic from horror gimmick to
theme. Two production notes: (1) the licensed songs (Daft Punk, etc.) are table-only —
the digital version needs originals or soundalikes, budget it; (2) the *personalization*
is the hard digital problem — the game must choose each character's song/fear from
observed play (dominant tags + behavior profile). That is exactly what the tag system and
the director architecture were built for; it's feasible, and it would be a marketing-grade
feature ("the game learns what your character fears") — but it's real work; scope it as
its own system.

### Sasha's F1 maze — **best-designed single encounter.**
The funnel-to-the-door structure (the demon herds her to Nikita's house), the two valid
paths (fight = shown revelation; flee = lonelier deduction, same truth), and the reveal
(the fear is inherited) — this respects player agency while keeping the emotional truth
fixed. The design principle embedded here (both paths reach the truth; they differ in
*cost and loneliness*) should be extracted as the project's template for personal
encounters.

### Incineradile intro — right thesis, right place.
The band-cage cinematic + the mycelium-puppet reveal teaches the series' actual thesis in
the tutorial: *nothing on this show is what it appears to be; the spectacle is a skin over
the real organism.* Which is also the Corporation, and also the game. Good.

## 5. The characters

**Sasha (cat, striker) — the emotional protagonist. Strongest character.** Her arc IS the
campaign's spine: stray → safety-over-strength choice → inherited fear → the maze → the
eventual Nikita encounters. Fully load-bearing, well-designed, no notes except: her arc's
payoff sits in floors 4–6 (see structural risk above).

**Nikita (NPC) — the deepest thing in the project, and its biggest tonal test.** The
reversion skill (the song comforts him AND resets him to 1945 — body restored, mind
re-broken) is a tragedy engine; the scarf is a perfect object; "contraband grief" as
mechanic is publishable-grade design. The Old/War identification puzzle fits the system's
information skills exactly. Three requirements: (1) design what *winning* a War Nikita
fight means — killing a confused old man on live TV is a tonal landmine, and it should be
— the audience metagame REACTING to it (does the crowd cheer?) is the series' thesis in
one encounter; decide the register on purpose. (2) The recognition asymmetry needs a
designed answer: can War Nikita ever *see* Sasha (a talking cat, through a wartime lens)?
That scene is the arc's climax; it deserves a written design, not improvisation. (3) Honor
the compendium's own guardrails (history as atmosphere; the wife gets personhood first).

**XQUEZ/T (AI, 7 K-pop personalities, glitching tank) — the best comedy asset with a
hidden knife.** The "Human" beat — the split-second awareness that it is ONE AI, not seven
— is genuine identity horror under the bit. Needs: the seven personalities individually
sketched (names, one trait each) or the gimmick stays a gimmick; the tag clusters (K-pop +
software blocks) show the system already loves this character. The tank kit gives the
comedy a body. High potential, currently underwritten.

**Mario (human brawler, Charm 5) — the show's favorite, and nobody has weaponized that.**
His song targets "the dream of feeling like a hero" — and he's the party member whose
desires are *most aligned with what the Corporation sells*. That's his tragedy hook: the
show will happily manufacture heroism for him as content, and the price is becoming
content. Currently the least-developed arc among the four; this one sentence of direction
would fix it.

**Filipe (sea lion, healer) — the enigma, deliberately or not.** Food-motivated puzzle
vibes on the surface; a Dissolution song about *godhood and superiority targeting cynical
pride* underneath — implying hidden contempt behind the wholesomeness. That contrast is
the most interesting unexploited character note in the party. His song is still TBD
(compendium open item #1) — resolving it will define him. Do it before floors 4–6.

**Frod (human, joined post-compendium) — undocumented.** No backstory, no song, no arc
hooks anywhere in the record. Flag: either he gets the same treatment as the others or
he'll be the character the story forgets.

## 6. The ending problem

**The campaign has no designed finale.** Mechanically a "Stage boss you're not expected to
beat" exists; the Winner's Arc (finish the show, go home, become a Patron) exists as
NG+ machinery. But narratively: what is the last episode? What does the Corporation *do*
when someone actually wins — and what does the audience do? The satire needs its final
statement (candidates latent in the material: the winner becomes the next product; the
audience revolts; the show is renewed forever; the colonization vote actually happens).
This doesn't need designing today — but it should be decided before Floor 3 content locks,
because the capital's convergence matrix (item #1 above) should be built pointing at it.

## Priority list (story work, in order of leverage)

1. **Design the F3 convergence matrix** (routes × offscreen resolutions → capital state) —
   it turns three routes into one story.
2. **Name the spine** ("what cures corruption, and what does it cost") and audit every beat
   against it — cheap, clarifying.
3. **Fix Medium's compliance assumption** (refusal forks) and **decide the brand paradox**
   (feature or bug).
4. **Create the production cast** (announcer + producer/showrunner) — the satire needs
   faces.
5. **Patch the continuity bug** (F3 farm leader's 170-year lifespan) and define the mask's
   rules and Nullrot's motive.
6. **Design Nikita's two key scenes** (what winning a War-fight means; the recognition
   scene) — before floors 4–6 exist, so the floors get built around them.
7. **Finish the party:** Filipe's song, XQUEZ/T's seven personalities, Mario's
   show-favorite arc, anything at all for Frod.
8. **Escort-to-arena reinvention for the Loong** (digital) + song licensing plan for the
   Dissolution encounter.
