## 1. Tradition overview — the casino angle

The Norse corpus is the most gambling-shaped major mythology in the census. Its gods wager
*heads* (Odin vs. Vafþrúðnir; Loki vs. the dwarf Brokkr), pledge *body parts* as collateral
(Tyr's hand), game *bounded grants* (Gefjon's plough-land), run *rigged tables*
(Útgarða-Loki's illusion-contests), and treat luck itself as a heritable, lendable asset
(hamingja). Above all: **this pantheon knows its own end date.** Ragnarök is scheduled,
the seating chart of who kills whom is published, and the gods bet anyway — which is the
Cosmic Casino's Forsaken psychology pre-installed. Proposed pantheon-level modern-life
attribution (needs owner sign-off, in the spirit of canon §4's Hawaii-Greeks): **the Norse
table plays doomsday-prepper energy** — bug-out bags under the seats, generational wealth
in fetters and escrow, betting heavy precisely because the collapse date is circled on the
calendar.

Source picture: source_quality 5 — the whole primary corpus exists in public-domain
translation: Poetic Edda (Bellows 1923), Prose Edda (Brodeur 1916), Völsunga saga,
Beowulf, Saxo, Tacitus. Revival worship (Ásatrú; Ásatrúarfélagið state-recognized in
Iceland since 1973) puts the core pantheon at influence 3 — the rubric's own named anchor.
Art-direction caution carried over from census: avoid the specific white-supremacist
co-opted renderings (othala/sowilo runes, "black sun," valknut-as-insignia) and all Marvel
trade dress (Thor/Loki/Odin/Hela costume designs); the myth figures themselves are PD.

## 2. Verification record (honesty note)

Direct WebFetch of sacred-texts.com, Wikisource, and Mythopedia returned HTTP 403 through
the session proxy. Verification therefore ran through **search-result quotation
cross-checks**: multiple independent hosts reproducing the exact Bellows/Brodeur wordings
for every load-bearing stanza, corroborated against Wikipedia-as-index one level down.
Claims verified this session (exact passages quoted in results):

- **Vafþrúðnismál 19** — the wisdom contest is played for the contestants' heads: "Here in the hall our heads, O guest, / Shall we wager our wisdom upon" (Bellows). [wikisource/sacred-texts snippets]
- **Grímnismál 14** — Freyja's half of the slain: "The half of the dead each day does she choose, / And half does Othin have" (Bellows). [Fólkvangr — Wikipedia; wikisource]
- **Völuspá 20** — the Norns: "Laws they made there, and life allotted / To the sons of men, and set their fates"; **Völuspá 28** — Odin's eye pledged in Mímir's well (Bellows; Mimisbrunnr.info comparative edition).
- **Gylfaginning 1 / Ynglinga saga 5** — Gefjon: Gylfi grants "one plough-land in his kingdom, as much as four oxen could plow up in a day and night"; she yokes four giant-son oxen and ploughs Zealand out. **A gamed grant, not a coin-flip wager** — census one-liner corrected. [Gefjon — Wikipedia; Young trans.]
- **Skáldskaparmál (Brodeur ch. XXXV region)** — Loki "wagered his head" with Brokkr; verdict for the dwarves; "the head, not the neck" dodge; lips sewn. [Brokkr — Wikipedia; norse-mythology.org]
- **Gylfaginning 34 (Brodeur numbering)** — Gleipnir's six impossible things; Tyr's hand pledged in Fenrir's mouth; "everyone laughed except Týr, who there lost his right hand." Also Hel's appointment over nine worlds, her hall Éljúðnir, dish Hunger, knife Famine, half blue-black half flesh. [Fenrir, Gleipnir, Hel — Wikipedia; sacred-texts index]
- **Gylfaginning 44–47** — Útgarða-Loki's rigged contests and their referents (Logi=wildfire, Hugi=thought, horn=sea, cat=Jörmungandr, Elli=old age). [Útgarða-Loki — Wikipedia; norse-mythology.org]
- **Gylfaginning 49** — Hel's ransom terms for Baldr ("if all things in the world, alive or dead, weep for him...") and Þökk's "let Hel hold what she has." [Hermóðr, Þökk — Wikipedia]
- **Þrymskviða** — Freyja's wrathful snort, the hall shaking, Brísingamen bursting; Thor's bride disguise. [Þrymskviða — Wikipedia; JSTOR Daily]
- **Fáfnismál / Völsunga saga** — heart-blood taste → bird-speech; the birds expose Reginn; the cursed hoard warning. [Fáfnismál — Wikipedia; OLLI/GMU trans.]
- **Beowulf** — the weaponless boast against Grendel and its fulfillment; death in the dragon fight with Wiglaf. [LitCharts/SparkNotes line summaries]
- **Hamingja** — luck as personified, heritable, and LENDABLE (Hjalti Skeggiason borrows King Olaf's hamingja for a voyage); parallels to fylgja noted by Orchard and Simek. [Hamingja — Wikipedia]
- **Lokasenna 21** — Odin on Gefjun: "For the fate that is set for all she sees, / Even as I, methinks" (Bellows; Bellows' own footnote flags that the line fits Frigg better — recorded as a caveat).

## 3. Entities

### Odin (`norse_germanic_odin`) — god, patron, VVIP
The corpus's compulsive all-in player: an eye pledged in Mímir's well for one drink of
wisdom (Völuspá 28; Gylfaginning 15); nine nights hanged on the world-tree, "myself to
myself," to seize the runes (Hávamál 138–141); his head wagered on a riddle-contest with
the giant Vafþrúðnir (Vafþrúðnismál 19, won at 55 with the unanswerable "what did Odin
whisper in Baldr's ear"); the mead of poetry stolen by seduction and eagle-flight
(Skáldskaparmál). He knows Fenrir swallows him at Ragnarök and drafts dead champions
anyway — the Forsaken psychology in person.
**Axes:** generosity 3 (gives runes, victory, mead — always priced); strictness 4
(exacting sacrifices); pettiness 2 (plays centuries-long games); wrath 3 (patient, lethal);
fidelity 2 (notoriously abandons champions when their death serves the Ragnarök draft —
Lokasenna 22's accusation of giving victory unfairly); risk_appetite 5 (see: everything
above). Matches the spec §2.2 worked example exactly — treated as an anchor.
**Myth leads:** `myth_norse_germanic_odin_eye_pledge` (Völuspá 28; Gylfaginning 15),
`myth_norse_germanic_odin_hanging_runes` (Hávamál 138–145),
`myth_norse_germanic_odin_head_wager` (Vafþrúðnismál),
`myth_norse_germanic_mead_of_poetry` (Skáldskaparmál),
`myth_norse_germanic_ragnarok` (Völuspá 44–58; Gylfaginning 51).

### Thor (`norse_germanic_thor`) — god, patron, VVIP
Giant-slayer and Midgard's insurance policy (Gylfaginning 21: hammer, strength-belt, iron
gloves). Played every game at Útgarða-Loki's rigged table to the end (Gylfaginning 44–47);
hooked the World Serpent on an ox-head and was a heartbeat from landing it (Hymiskviða;
Gylfaginning 48); recovered his stolen hammer in bridal veil and necklace, then killed the
whole wedding (Þrymskviða). Dies at Ragnarök nine steps after killing Jörmungandr — venom,
not violence, takes him (Gylfaginning 51).
**Axes:** generosity 4 (unpaid guardian of farmers and gods alike); strictness 2 (few
rules: show up, fight); pettiness 2 (anger without grudges); wrath 4 (hammer-first
diplomacy, Þrymskviða's massacre); fidelity 5 (never once abandons his side); risk_appetite
4 (accepts every contest, even visibly rigged ones — §5: eager all-in candidate).
**Myth leads:** `myth_norse_germanic_utgard_contests`, `myth_norse_germanic_thor_fishing`,
`myth_norse_germanic_thrym_bride_heist`, `myth_norse_germanic_ragnarok`.

### Loki (`norse_germanic_loki`) — god, patron/dealer, VIP
Wagered his own head against the dwarf smiths on whether Brokkr and Sindri could out-forge
the Sons of Ívaldi — lost, then lawyered the payout ("the head is yours; the neck is not")
and had his lips sewn shut for it (Skáldskaparmál). Engineered the one successful
god-unmaking in the corpus (Baldr, via the mistletoe and — as Þökk — the single refused
tear: Gylfaginning 49), roasted the entire pantheon at a feast (Lokasenna), invented the
fishing net and was caught as a salmon with it, and is bound under dripping venom with a
son's entrails until doomsday (Gylfaginning 50).
**Axes:** generosity 2 (his gifts exist to cover his own liabilities); strictness 1;
pettiness 5 (Lokasenna is one long roast; cut Sif's hair for fun); wrath 3 (vindictive
when cornered, not hot); fidelity 1 (betrays every side, ends against the gods at
Ragnarök); risk_appetite 5 (his head, twice over).
**Myth leads:** `myth_norse_germanic_loki_head_wager`,
`myth_norse_germanic_baldr_death_ransom`, `myth_norse_germanic_loki_binding`,
`myth_norse_germanic_lokasenna`, `myth_norse_germanic_ragnarok`.

### Freyja (`norse_germanic_freyja`) — god, patron/investor, VIP
Vanir goddess of love, seiðr, war, and liquid wealth. Holds a standing revenue share on
every battlefield: "the half of the dead each day does she choose, and half does Othin
have" (Grímnismál 14 — verified). Taught seiðr-magic to the Aesir (Ynglinga saga 4); weeps
tears of red gold searching for her lost husband Óðr (Gylfaginning 35); her refusal-snort
in Þrymskviða shakes the hall and bursts Brísingamen. Cat-drawn chariot and boar
Hildisvíni attested (Gylfaginning 24; Hyndluljóð).
**Axes:** generosity 3 (grants love, wealth, magic — but keeps accounts); strictness 3;
pettiness 3 (the Þrymskviða fury; Hyndluljóð needling); wrath 3; fidelity 4 (weeps gold
across the world for one husband; keeps her chosen dead forever); risk_appetite 3 (pays
steep prices for treasures, but doesn't bet the estate).
**Myth leads:** `myth_norse_germanic_freyja_od_tears` (Gylfaginning 35),
`myth_norse_germanic_thrym_bride_heist` (Þrymskviða — her role).

### Tyr (`norse_germanic_tyr`) — god, patron, VIP
The god of law who made the gods' one honest payment: when Fenrir demanded collateral
against trickery, Tyr put his sword-hand in the wolf's mouth knowing the bind was a lie,
and when it held, "everyone laughed except Týr, who there lost his right hand"
(Gylfaginning 34 — verified). Kennings: "the one-handed god," "fosterer of the wolf."
Sigrdrífumál 6 ties him to victory-runes ("name Týr twice"). Fights Garm to mutual death
at Ragnarök. Tuesday still carries his name — the continuity that buys influence 2.
**Axes:** generosity 2 (austere); strictness 5 (law personified); pettiness 1 (paid
without complaint); wrath 2 (measured); fidelity 5 (the collateral CLEARED); risk_appetite
3 (catastrophic stakes accepted as duty, not thrill).
**Myth leads:** `myth_norse_germanic_fenrir_binding`, `myth_norse_germanic_ragnarok`.

### Hel (`norse_germanic_hel`) — god, patron/dealer, VIP
Loki's daughter, cast by Odin into Niflheim and given authority over nine worlds to
apportion lodging to all who die of sickness and age (Gylfaginning 34 — verified). Her
balance sheet is her portrait: hall Éljúðnir ("Sleet-Cold"), dish Hunger, knife Famine,
threshold Pit-of-Stumbling; she herself half blue-black, half flesh. The Baldr ransom
(Gylfaginning 49 — verified) is the casino's model escrow: an absolute stated condition,
one holdout (Þökk: "let Hel hold what she has"), pot kept, no appeal.
**Axes:** generosity 1 (her table settings are Hunger and Famine); strictness 5 (one tear
short = deal void); pettiness 2 (impersonal); wrath 2 (never rages — keeps); fidelity 4
(her stated terms are iron in both directions); risk_appetite 1 (the house never gambles).
**Myth leads:** `myth_norse_germanic_baldr_death_ransom`.

### Gefjon (`norse_germanic_gefjon`) — god, patron, Normal (rare-board collector)
King Gylfi comps a traveling woman "one plough-land, as much as four oxen could plow up in
a day and night"; she fetches four oxen from Jötunheimr — her sons by a giant — and the
plough cuts so deep it tears the land itself loose; she drags it into the sound and names
it Zealand (Gylfaginning 1; Ynglinga saga 5 — verified). **Correction logged:** a gamed
grant, not a wager — she beat the house's literal wording, which is better. Lokasenna 21
adds a sleeper credential: Odin says she sees all fate "even as I" (Bellows' footnote
suspects confusion with Frigg — caveat recorded). Counted among the Asynjur; Ynglinga saga
has Odin dispatch her — hence `court_of`.
**Axes:** generosity 3 (a land-shaping abundance goddess); strictness 2; pettiness 2;
wrath 2; fidelity 3; risk_appetite 4 (bets big, but only after rigging her own odds —
four oxen were never going to be ordinary oxen).
**Myth leads:** `myth_norse_germanic_gefjon_ploughing`, `myth_norse_germanic_lokasenna`.

### Sigurd (`norse_germanic_sigurd`) — hero, contestant_legend, Normal
Kills Fáfnir from a trench beneath the dragon's path (tactic advised by a disguised old
man — Odin; Völsunga saga 18); burns his thumb on the roasting heart, tastes the
heart-blood, instantly understands bird-speech; the birds expose Reginn's planned
betrayal, and Sigurd takes head and hoard both (Fáfnismál — verified). The hoard carries
Andvari's curse (Reginsmál) and destroys him and two dynasties. Norse Sigurd = continental
Siegfried (Nibelungenlied) — grouped `sigurd_siegfried`.
**Axes:** generosity 3; strictness 3; pettiness 1; wrath 3; fidelity 4 (his betrayal of
Brynhild is potion-induced — the saga insists on it); risk_appetite 4 (fights dragons from
arm's length below).
**Myth leads:** `myth_norse_germanic_sigurd_fafnir` (Fáfnismál; Völsunga saga 13–20).

### Beowulf (`norse_germanic_beowulf`) — hero, contestant_legend, Normal
Announces he will meet Grendel without weapons because Grendel uses none — a sportsmanship
boast the poem frames as deliberate — and makes it good, tearing the fiend's arm from its
socket (Beowulf ll. ~430–835 — verified). Fifty years later, an old king, he takes the
dragon with one loyal second (Wiglaf), wins, and dies of the winning (ll. 2200–3182).
**Axes:** generosity 4 (a ring-giving lord); strictness 3; pettiness 1; wrath 2; fidelity
5 (stands when the comitatus runs); risk_appetite 5 (bare hands by choice; the last board
at retirement age).
**Myth leads:** `myth_norse_germanic_beowulf_grendel`, `myth_norse_germanic_beowulf_dragon`.

### Fenrir (`norse_germanic_fenrir`) — beast, table_boss, Normal
Loki's wolf-son, raised among the gods (only Tyr dared feed him), growing so fast the
prophecies panicked the pantheon into three binding attempts — he broke Leyding and Dromi
for sport, then took the suspicious silk ribbon only against a pledged hand (Gylfaginning
34 — verified). Bound with a sword propping his jaws, drooling the river Ván, until he
swallows Odin whole at Ragnarök and dies to Víðarr a breath later (Gylfaginning 51).
**Axes:** wrath 5 / risk_appetite 4 (accepted each bind as a challenge from pride);
fidelity 3 — note the inversion: HE honored the collateral contract precisely (default →
hand taken); it was the gods who welched. Pettiness 3; generosity 1; strictness 2.
**Myth leads:** `myth_norse_germanic_fenrir_binding`, `myth_norse_germanic_ragnarok`.

### Jörmungandr (`norse_germanic_jormungandr`) — beast, table_boss, Normal
Thrown into the world-sea as a hatchling, grew to circle Midgard and grip his own tail
(Gylfaginning 34) — the ouroboros IS the map boundary. Thor has three cracks at him:
lifted as the disguised cat in Útgarð (verified), hooked on the ox-head and lost when the
line was cut (Gylfaginning 48; Hymiskviða), and finally kills and is killed by him —
nine steps of venom — at Ragnarök (Gylfaginning 51).
**Axes:** wrath 4 (a three-myth grudge); strictness 3 (one iron rule: the tail-grip);
risk_appetite 2 (waits an age to strike once); fidelity 3; pettiness 2; generosity 1.
**Myth leads:** `myth_norse_germanic_thor_fishing`, `myth_norse_germanic_utgard_contests`,
`myth_norse_germanic_ragnarok`.

### Útgarða-Loki (`norse_germanic_utgarda_loki`) — fiend, table_boss/forsaken_host, Normal
The illusionist giant-king who ran myth's most explicit rigged casino (Gylfaginning 44–47
— verified): Loki out-eaten by Wildfire, Þjálfi out-run by Thought, Thor drinking the
literal ocean, lifting the World Serpent dressed as a housecat, wrestled to one knee by
Old Age herself — every contest honest-looking, every opponent a disguised universal. He
confesses the frame-story afterward and vanishes, castle and all, before the hammer lands.
**Axes:** risk_appetite 4 (he invited THOR in to con him); pettiness 3 (humiliation is the
product); wrath 1 (never fights — vanishes); generosity 2 (hospitable to a point);
fidelity 2; strictness 3 (his games have rules; the rules are the trap).
**Myth leads:** `myth_norse_germanic_utgard_contests`.

### The Norns (`norse_germanic_norns`) — spirit, dealer, Normal
Urðr, Verðandi, Skuld at the well beneath the world-tree: "Laws they made there, and life
allotted / To the sons of men, and set their fates" (Völuspá 20 — verified; Gylfaginning
15 — they water the tree daily; Helgakviða Hundingsbana I shows them weaving a newborn
hero's fate-threads). No cult, no appeals process, no exceptions — even the gods ride the
odds the Norns post.
**Axes:** strictness 5 / fidelity 5 / risk_appetite 1 (they ARE the line); wrath 1,
pettiness 1 (nothing personal — ever); generosity 2 (some threads are golden;
Helgakviða's hero gets a bright one).
**Myth leads:** `myth_norse_germanic_norns_allotment`.

### Hamingja (`norse_germanic_hamingja`) — spirit (concept-class), Normal, mythic rarity
Not a person but the Norse theory of luck: a semi-personified fortune that attends an
individual or family line, INHERITED at death (following a beloved kinsman for
generations), visible in dreams as a guardian woman or animal, and — decisive for the
casino — LENDABLE: Hjalti Skeggiason asks King Olaf to lend him his hamingja for a
dangerous voyage (attested; see verification record). Simek and Orchard link it to the
fylgja complex. No narrative myth exists, so no myth_refs — saga attestations only
(Víga-Glúms saga's dream-woman; Vatnsdæla saga's family luck), honestly recorded.
**Axes:** generosity 4 (it exists to bless); fidelity 4 (generational — yet it can leave
or be lent, hence not 5); wrath 1 (bad luck is its absence, not its anger); strictness 2;
pettiness 1; risk_appetite 2.

### Gleipnir (`norse_germanic_gleipnir`) — artifact, Normal
The fetter dwarves forged from six things that do not exist — the footfall of a cat, the
beard of a woman, the roots of a mountain, the sinews of a bear, the breath of a fish, the
spittle of a bird — silk-thin, and the only thing in creation that holds Fenrir; the more
he strains, the harder it grips (Gylfaginning 34 — verified). Deployment price: Tyr's
right hand. Personality axes are nominal per schema — recorded as item behavior (absolute
strictness/fidelity, zero appetite).
**Myth leads:** `myth_norse_germanic_fenrir_binding`.
