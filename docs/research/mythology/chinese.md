## Tradition overview — the casino angle

Chinese folk religion is the casino's dream client base: it is a LIVING major tradition
(hundreds of millions of practitioners) whose native theology is *already* a celestial
corporation. Heaven is a civil service — gods hold offices, file reports, get promoted
and demoted by the Jade Emperor's HR department, and several of them (Caishen, Tudi Gong,
Hei Bai Wuchang) are petitioned *today, in the real world, for lottery numbers*. No other
Wave-2 tradition hands the Cosmic Casino this much pre-built material: the modern-life
attribution practically writes itself — **the Chinese pantheon plays the table as a vast
government bureau**: org charts, official chops, performance reviews, and a collections
department with excellent manners.

The tradition is also unusually rich in **Forsaken material**: gods who are beheaded and
keep fighting (Xingtian), gods who dismember themselves to settle debts (Nezha), a wealth
god who died backing a losing side (Zhao Gongming/Caishen), a demon-queller whose godhood
began with a protest suicide (Zhong Kui), and the greatest contestant of all time, who
went all-in against a Buddha and lost (Sun Wukong). Nine of the census's fourteen
luck/forsaken flags are covered in this batch; the five that didn't fit are listed in the
skip notes.

**Cost of ruling D-4:** this tradition pays the highest D-4 tax of any family — Guan Yu,
Mazu, Wong Tai Sin (a literal luck cult), Ji Gong, and four of the Eight Immortals are
excluded as deified historical humans (see census `closed_material_notes`). The roster
below is what remains after that honest cut, and it is still deep.

## Jade Emperor (chinese_jade_emperor)

**Citations.** Werner 1922 ch. on the Jade Emperor; Wikipedia "Jade Emperor" (index) with
worship verified one level down: SCMP, "Why for some Chinese, ninth day of Lunar New Year
is the most important: it's the day they worship the Jade Emperor" (Bai Tian Gong /
Tian Gong Dan, Hokkien practice); jinpaper.com Tian Gong Dan ritual guide. Zodiac Great
Race attribution verified: chinesenewyear.net "Zodiac origin story", Wikipedia "Chinese
zodiac". Court-under-siege: JTTW chs. 4–7.

**Personality rationale.** Generosity 2: rewards flow by protocol, not largesse.
Strictness 5: the celestial law code is the whole point of him. Pettiness 3: his court
demotes gods for single banquet infractions (Zhu Bajie's exile). Wrath 3: measured but
escalates to armies (the hosts sent against Wukong). Fidelity 4: mandates are honored;
the bureaucracy is stable. Risk 1: he is the house, not a gambler.

**Myth leads (Wave 3).** `myth_chinese_zodiac_race` (folk_tale; the Great Race — a
ready-made game format) · `myth_chinese_havoc_in_heaven` (heroic_epic; his palace
besieged, shared with Sun Wukong).

**Syncretic note.** Declared group `sakra_jade_emperor`: Chinese Buddhism identifies the
Jade Emperor with Śakra/Indra — Wave 4 should reconcile with any Buddhist-tradition Śakra
record.

## Caishen (chinese_caishen)

**Citations.** Wikipedia "Zhao Gongming" (index); effigy-death account verified one level
down (Britannica-derived text at howardchoy.wordpress.com and heathenchinese.wordpress.com:
Jiang Ziya's straw effigy, twenty days of incantation, peachwood arrow through the heart
— the Nail-Head Seven Arrows rite of FSYY chs. 46–51, taught by Lu Ya); FSYY posthumous
investiture as Lord of the Dark Altar (Xuantan Zhenjun) with four wealth-attendant
subordinates; iconography (iron whip, black tiger) same sources. Worship scale: Wave-1
census sources (World History Encyclopedia; Pew folk-religion estimates) — Caishen
veneration at Lunar New Year (welcoming the wealth god on the fifth day, firecrackers)
spans well over 100M practitioners.

**Personality rationale.** Generosity 4: the gift-giving god par excellence. Strictness
2 / pettiness 2: money forgives. Wrath 2: he punishes by withdrawal, not fire. Fidelity
2: capital follows returns — lowest buy-out resistance on the roster, exactly right for
a wealth god (deal-sheet semantics §5). Risk 4: a hermit who rode a black tiger into a
dynastic war for a friend and died for it — an eager Forsaken host per the §5 amendment.

**Forsaken lens.** Zhao Gongming is a god who went all-in on the losing side, was unmade
by sorcery, and got re-capitalized post-mortem by the Investiture. Flagged in the record.

**Myth leads.** `myth_chinese_zhao_gongming_effigy_death` (heroic_epic; FSYY).

**D-4 note.** The composite Caishen persona is mythological; regional identifications
with historical humans (Bi Gan, Guan Yu as military wealth god) are NOT collected —
census made the same call.

**Myth leads.** `myth_chinese_tudi_zhang_fude_origin` (local_legend grade — folk

## Xiwangmu (chinese_xiwangmu)

**Citations.** Shanhaijing form verified: chinaknowledge.de "Xiwangmu" (tiger fangs,
leopard tail, presides over the catastrophes of the sky and the five destructive forces,
wu can 五殘). King Mu's visit: chinaknowledge.de "Mu Tianzi zhuan" + Wikipedia "Tale of
King Mu, Son of Heaven" (feast at the Jade Pool, Kunlun). Peach Banquet and its ruin:
JTTW ch. 5. Living cult verified: Wikipedia "Queen Mother of the West" + Guandu Temple
(kuantu.org.tw) + academic material on Cihui Tang — 900+ branch halls in Taiwan (2003
statistics) venerating her as Yaochi Jinmu, plus Hualien Shengan Temple.

**Personality rationale.** Generosity 3: she grants the elixir and hosts the banquet —
for the invited. Strictness 4: the guest list is the theology. Pettiness 2: offenses
against her are answered institutionally, not spitefully. Wrath 3: her archaic form
governs plagues and sky-punishments. Fidelity 4: millennial constancy. Risk 3: she owns
the rarest asset in the cosmos (immortality) and meters it out carefully.

**Myth leads.** `myth_chinese_king_mu_visits_xiwangmu` (local_legend/heroic_epic; Mu
Tianzi Zhuan) · `myth_chinese_wukong_ruins_peach_banquet` (heroic_epic; JTTW ch. 5,
shared with Sun Wukong). The Peaches of Immortality (census artifact, skipped for slots)
are folded into her record as an item seed.

## Nüwa (chinese_nuwa)

**Citations.** Sky-mending: Huainanzi ch. 6 (Lanming) — pillars broken, five-colored
stones smelted, tortoise legs as new pillars; verified via Wikipedia "Nüwa" (index) and
newworldencyclopedia.org one level down. Creation of humans from clay: attested in Han
sources (Fengsu Tongyi; the molding motif already queried in the Chuci) — verified same
index. FSYY ch. 1: King Zhou (Di Xin) writes an erotic poem on her temple wall; she
summons the nine-tailed fox spirit to destroy his dynasty — verified (Wikipedia
Nüwa/FSYY summaries). Living worship: Wa Huang Gong at Shexian (Hebei), a major temple
fair — census-level claim, regional in scale.

**Personality rationale.** Generosity 4: made humanity, mended the sky for everyone.
Strictness 2: few demands. Pettiness 3: a single lewd poem cost a dynasty — divine
pettiness by any human measure, though she'd file it as blasphemy enforcement. Wrath 3:
patient, total, delegated. Fidelity 5: the mother who repairs rather than abandons.
Risk 2: a fixer, not a gambler.

**Myth leads.** `myth_chinese_nuwa_mends_the_sky` (world_myth) ·
`myth_chinese_nuwa_molds_humanity` (world_myth) · `myth_chinese_nuwa_summons_the_fox`
(heroic_epic; FSYY frame story). Fuxi (consort/brother) and Gonggong (whose pillar-
breaking necessitated the repair) are not in this batch — relations left for Wave 4.

## Zhong Kui (chinese_zhong_kui)

**Citations.** Origin account: chinaknowledge.de "Zhong Kui" — Shen Kuo (1031–1095),
*Bu mengxi bitan*: Emperor Xuanzong's fever dream, the large ghost who devours the small
ghost Xuhao, self-identified as Zhong Kui, the examinee who failed (military exam in
that version; civil in later tellings), died on the palace steps, and vowed to clear the
empire of evils; Wu Daozi paints the dream image. "Zhong Kui marries off his sister"
(New Year's Eve procession, demon-slaying sword as dowry): verified as a standard folk/
opera subject (chinaknowledge.de + Sotheby's "The Return of Zhong Kui" survey). Living
practice: door-print talismans, Duanwu exorcism performance — same sources.

**Personality rationale.** Generosity 3: protects households gratis. Strictness 4:
implacable against demons and cheats. Pettiness 3: his entire divine career is a
grievance kept warm. Wrath 4: he *eats* offenders. Fidelity 5: the sister-wedding legend
and his posthumous service both run on kept promises. Risk 4: staked his life on one
exam and his afterlife on one vow — an eager Forsaken host for the wronged.

**Myth leads.** `myth_chinese_zhong_kui_dream_of_xuanzong` (local_legend→heroic_epic;
the apotheosis) · `myth_chinese_zhong_kui_marries_off_sister` (folk_tale).

## Nezha (chinese_nezha)

**Citations.** FSYY chs. 12–14: kills Ao Bing, third son of the East Sea Dragon King;
to spare his family the dragons' retribution he carves his flesh and returns it to his
mother, his bones to his father; his teacher Taiyi Zhenren rebuilds him from lotus root
and gives the fire-tipped spear and wind-fire wheels — verified (Wikipedia "Nezha" +
chinese-mythology.com/mythsrevealed summaries of the FSYY text). Living cult verified:
taiwanese-gods.org "Nezha (Third Prince)" (Zhongtan Yuanshuai, protection deity);
Taiwan Panorama "Summoning the Divine — Celebrating the Third Prince's Birthday"
(Sanfeng Temple Kaohsiung pilgrimages); Wikipedia "Electric-Techno Neon Gods" +
ijih.org "Marshal Nezha 'transformed': Techno Nezha in Taiwan".

**Personality rationale.** Generosity 3. Strictness 2: rules bore him. Pettiness 3:
pulled a dragon's tendon over a grievance. Wrath 5: kills a dragon prince in a
playground quarrel; nearly wars on his own father. Fidelity 3: severed the parental
bond in the most literal way possible, then guarded the family altar forever — loyalty
with him is renegotiated, not assumed. Risk 5: his signature myth is staking his own
body. Textbook Forsaken host.

**Myth leads.** `myth_chinese_nezha_slays_ao_bing` (heroic_epic) ·
`myth_chinese_nezha_lotus_rebirth` (heroic_epic; the self-unmaking + rebuild).

**IP caution.** The current Nezha animated-film franchise designs are protected; the
deity, cult, and Ming corpus are public material. Census carries the same note.

## Xingtian (chinese_xingtian)

**Citations.** Shanhaijing, Classic of Overseas West: Xingtian contended with the
Supreme God (Di / the Yellow Emperor) for supremacy; beheaded, his head buried at
Changyang Mountain; nipples became eyes, navel a mouth; he dances with axe (qi) and
shield (gan) — verified (Wikipedia "Xingtian" + chinaknowledge-tier summaries). Tao
Qian (Tao Yuanming, 365–427), "Reading the Shanhaijing": "Xingtian dances with shield
and axe; his fierce will endures forever" — verified same sources.

**Personality rationale.** Generosity 2. Strictness 3. Pettiness 4: four millennia of
grudge is the resume. Wrath 5 / fidelity 5 / risk 5: he is one myth, and the myth is
never-yield — the purest deal-sheet in the batch (boons only at death's door).

**Myth leads.** `myth_chinese_xingtian_dances_with_axe_and_shield` (world_myth-adjacent
heroic_epic; the defiance itself).

**Honesty note.** One attested myth, no cult, influence 1 — a deliberately small entry.
He is in the batch because the §5 Forsaken amendment asks for exactly him: a god whose
existence-stake is already on the table.

## Sun Wukong (chinese_sun_wukong)

**Citations.** JTTW (1592; PD translations): ch. 3 death-ledger erasure; ch. 5 Peach
Banquet ruined; chs. 4–7 havoc in heaven; ch. 7 the wager with the Buddha — leap from

Mountain and 500 years under it (verified: Wikipedia "Sun Wukong",

1: rules are suggestions. Pettiness 4: leveled Heaven over a snubbed party invitation.
Wrath 4. Fidelity 4: pre-fillet loyalty is tribal, post-fillet loyalty is absolute —
he never abandons the pilgrimage even when banished. Risk 5: bet the throne of Heaven
on one somersault.

**Myth leads.** `myth_chinese_havoc_in_heaven` · `myth_chinese_buddha_palm_wager` (the
house's favorite cautionary all-in — grade heroic_epic, spectacle 5) ·
`myth_chinese_wukong_erases_death_ledger` · `myth_chinese_wukong_ruins_peach_banquet` ·

**Class note.** Hero per census and spec §2.2 (patron_capable false for non-god

## Baigujing (chinese_baigujing)

**Citations.** JTTW ch. 27 ("The cadaver demon three times mocks Tang Sanzang") —
the skeleton spirit's three disguises (village girl with poisoned food, her mother,

**Personality rationale.** Fidelity 1 / generosity 1: she is deception incarnate.

**Myth leads.** `myth_chinese_three_strikes_white_bone_demon` (heroic_epic episode;

## Hei Bai Wuchang (chinese_hei_bai_wuchang)

**Citations.** Wikipedia "Heibai Wuchang" (index), verified one level down via
utkasianreligions.org "Heibai Wuchang: Guardians of the Underworld" and
journeytothewestresearch.com "Monkey and the Summoners of Hell: The Story and Origins
of the Heibai Wuchang": the constables Xie Bi'an (tall, white, hat inscribed with
fortune-at-first-sight variants) and Fan Wujiu (short, dark); separated while escorting
a convict, they agree to meet under a bridge; Fan drowns in the flood rather than leave
the appointed spot; Xie hangs himself in grief; the Jade Emperor, moved by their
faithfulness, appoints them escorts of the dead. Fortune-deity role verified: "they
grant riches and winning lottery numbers to help men in need or those of virtue"
(Wikipedia, sourced) — the SE-Asian Tua Di Ya Pek 4D-number practice corroborates;
Taiwan's Qiye/Baye temple processions (Bao'an-type City God temples) are the living
cult base.

**Personality rationale.** Fidelity 5 is the whole myth — they died of keeping an
appointment. Strictness 4: the arrest happens on schedule. Generosity 3: tips for the
respectful. Wrath 3: the disrespectful meet the Black Guard's placard. Risk 3.

**Myth leads.** `myth_chinese_wuchang_bridge_promise` (folk_tale/local_legend; the
oath kept unto death — justice_oaths made flesh).

## Meng Po (chinese_meng_po)

**Citations.** First textual appearance in the Qing *Jade Record* (Yuli Baochao), the
popular Buddhist-Daoist morality text: a pious woman of the Han era appointed to brew
the five-flavored broth of forgetfulness at the Naihe Bridge (Bridge of Helplessness),
erasing every soul's memories before reincarnation — verified (Wikipedia "Meng Po";
The China Project, "Hell's bittersweet end: Meng Po, goddess of forgetfulness";
longhumountain.com Daoist account). Her legend-figure origin (an anonymous pious
woman) is legendary, not a historical person — D-4-clean.

**Personality rationale.** Strictness 5: essentially no soul passes undrunk.
Generosity 3: the soup is free, and arguably a mercy — the price is hidden. Pettiness
1 / wrath 1: nothing personal, ever. Fidelity 5: one post, held since the Han.
Risk 1: the house never gambles at the exit ramp.

**Judgment call (influence 2).** Belief in her office is universal wherever Chinese
afterlife belief lives, but direct cult is nearly nil — she is BELIEVED, not
worshipped. Influence 2 (strong continuity, no organized worship of her own) rather
than 3; ±1 defensible either way against the Hei Bai Wuchang comparison (who do have
temples and processions, hence their 3).

**Myth leads.** `myth_chinese_meng_po_soup` (world_myth-grade cosmological office —
every soul that ever lived passes her station).
