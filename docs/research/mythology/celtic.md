## Tradition overview — and the casino angle

The Celtic family is three unequal sub-corpora. **Irish** is deep and public-domain-rich: the Mythological Cycle (Cath Maige Tuired, Lebor Gabála Érenn), the Ulster Cycle (Táin Bó Cúailnge), and the Fenian Cycle, plus a living folklore layer (banshee, leprechaun) collected from Croker (1825) onward. **Welsh** is literary but genuinely mythological: the Four Branches of the Mabinogi and Culhwch and Olwen (Charlotte Guest's PD translation). **Gaulish** is epigraphy and iconography only — no native narrative survives; Cernunnos is handled at exactly that honesty level below.

Casino frame: this pantheon's table culture is an Irish wedding that turns into a wake and back again — hospitality is enforceable law, satire is a weapon that draws literal blood (Bres was deposed by a poem), every second god founded a games festival, and the wagering runs through livestock, board games, and blood-prices. Lugh — inventor of fidchell, founder of the Tailteann funeral games — is the flagged luck_gambling nobleman. Modern-life attribution (pantheon caricature, per canon §4): the Tuatha Dé play the casino like the festival-and-racetrack circuit — poets in the commentary box, a feud ledger nobody forgets, and the buffet god ordering everything twice.

Forsaken lens: this tradition is unusually rich in unmaking stories — Nuada loses throne-with-arm and claws both back; Bres wagers his life on successive bids and survives on the fourth; Lleu is killed through an assembled loophole, rots as an eagle, and is sung back. All three census forsaken_candidate flags are extracted below.

Living-worship note: organized revival is real (Neo-Druidry, Celtic Reconstructionism; the Coru Cathubodua priesthood for the Morrígan; flamekeeping cells for Brigid — Kildare flame relit 1993, Ord Brighideach) — verified via corupriesthood.com, irishpaganschool.com, and Kildare/Brigidine coverage. Per depiction-policy v2 this is recorded as `depiction_risk: low` DATA, not a gate. Nothing in this family is culturally closed.

## The Morrígan

**Primary texts:** Táin Bó Cúailnge (Dunn tr. 1914, PD; Faraday 1904): she comes to Cú Chulainn as a young noblewoman offering her love and her wealth; refused, she vows to attack him at the ford and does — as an eel that trips him, a wolf that drives a stampede, a hornless red heifer leading it; he wounds her three times; she later appears as an old milking woman and extracts three healing blessings from him. Táin Bó Regamna (prelude) stages the same enmity. Cath Maige Tuired (Stokes tr. 1891, PD, CELT T300010): she meets the Dagda at the ford of the Unshin at Samhain (§84–85), promises the king of the Fomorians' blood, and speaks the double prophecy that closes the text — peace to heaven, then the world's unraveling. **Verification:** CELT edition confirmed; revival worship verified at corupriesthood.com (Coru Cathubodua FAQ + About the Morrígan) and irishpaganschool.com.

**Axis rationale:** generosity 2 (prophecy and victory-marking, rarely gifts); strictness 4 (her favor is conditional and withdrawn hard); pettiness 5 (an entire epic spent tormenting the hero who declined her); wrath 4 (threefold beast assault mid-duel); fidelity 3 (constant to the Tuatha Dé, fickle to individuals); risk_appetite 4 (war-gambler; eager Forsaken host per §5).

**Myth leads (Wave 3):** myth_celtic_morrigan_offer_and_ford_attacks (Táin; heroic_epic); myth_celtic_morrigan_ford_union (CMT §84; local_legend/world_myth-adjacent via the closing prophecy); myth_celtic_second_battle_mag_tuired (world_myth, shared).

## Lugh

**Primary texts:** Cath Maige Tuired §§53–74 (Stokes, PD): the doorkeeper test — Lugh names craft after craft (wright, smith, champion, harper, poet, sorcerer, physician, cupbearer, brazier), each already covered, until he asks whether any ONE man has them all; the fidchell boards of Tara are brought out and he wins all the stakes; admitted as Samildánach. CMT climax: kills Balor with a sling-stone through the eye. Oidheadh Chloinne Tuireann (Joyce, Old Celtic Romances 1879, PD): the merciless éraic (blood-price) quest he sets his father's killers, arriving on Manannán's steed Aonbharr in Manannán's mail; he refuses them the healing pig-skin as they die — the hound-whelp Failinis is among the fee. Lughnasadh: founded by Lugh as funeral games for his foster-mother Tailtiu (Metrical Dindshenchas, Gwynn tr., Tailtiu; LGE) — festival continuity to the present. Táin: Lugh reveals himself as Cú Chulainn's otherworld father and heals him through a three-day sleep (verified via Táin summaries citing the standard editions). Fidchell's invention is attributed to Lugh in the standard references (MacKillop, Dictionary of Celtic Mythology; fidchell histories index this attribution) — combined with the Tara stakes scene, the luck_gambling flag is earned twice over.

**Axis rationale:** generosity 3 (founds festivals, grants skills — but his gifts are standards); strictness 5 (the door test; mastery demanded); pettiness 3 (the éraic is legal, and pitiless); wrath 4 (Tuireann's sons die begging); fidelity 4 (avenges his father, honors his foster-mother forever, heals his son); risk_appetite 4 (walked at Balor's eye; stakes-play at Tara; games founder — eager Forsaken host).

**Myth leads:** myth_celtic_lugh_samildanach_gate; myth_celtic_second_battle_mag_tuired; myth_celtic_fate_children_of_tuireann; myth_celtic_lughnasadh_tailtiu; myth_celtic_lugh_heals_cu_chulainn.

## Brigid

**Primary texts:** Cath Maige Tuired §125 (Stokes, PD): her son Ruadán, sent to spy on and spear the smith Goibniu, is killed — Bríg comes, shrieks and weeps, and this is the first keening (caíned) heard in Ireland. Cormac's Glossary (Sanas Cormaic, tr. O'Donovan/Stokes, PD): Brigit daughter of the Dagda, the poetess, with two sisters of the same name — woman of healing and woman of smithcraft — 'whose name among all Irishmen a goddess was called Brigit.' **Living boundary:** the goddess is syncretized with St. Brigid of Kildare (feast Feb 1, now an Irish public holiday; perpetual flame kept at Kildare to the 16th c., relit 1993, tended by the Brigidine Sisters; Ord Brighideach flamekeeping cells worldwide — verified via brigidine.org.au and Kildare coverage). The SAINT is excluded per D-4; the goddess ships; art direction avoids Christian saint iconography. The rush cross is listed in iconography with this flag — it straddles the boundary.

**Axis rationale:** generosity 4 (healer-provider; the saint-lore's boundless charity bleeds into her folk image); strictness 3 (her fire sanctuary at Kildare was hedge-barred to men — Gerald of Wales, saint-side attestation, noted as boundary material); pettiness 1, wrath 2 (no vengeance stories at all); fidelity 4 (the flame is constancy itself); risk_appetite 2.

**Myth leads:** myth_celtic_brigid_first_keening (CMT §125; local_legend grade, spectacle high — grief as performance is very GPT).

## The Dagda

**Primary texts:** Cath Maige Tuired (Stokes, PD): §§88–93 — sent to delay the Fomorians, he is forced to eat a king's ransom of porridge from a ground-trench ladle 'big enough for a man and woman to lie in'; he eats it all, is mocked, waddles off — and the delay works. §84 — his Samhain union with the Morrígan at the ford. Final sections: with Lugh and Ogma he retrieves his harper and harp (Uaithne / Daur Dá Bláo) from the Fomorian hall — the harp flies to his hand killing nine, and he plays the three strains: weeping, laughter, sleep. His cauldron is one of the Four Treasures 'from which no company ever went away unsatisfied' (LGE / CMT §6); his club kills with one end and revives with the other (attested in the Dindshenchas and the Dagda material indexed in MacKillop).

**Axis rationale:** generosity 5 (the cauldron IS his character); strictness 2 (undignified, informal); pettiness 2; wrath 3 (nine dead per club-swing when roused); fidelity 3 (philanderer of record — Boann); risk_appetite 3 (walks alone into the enemy camp under truce, twice).

**Myth leads:** myth_celtic_dagda_porridge (heroic_epic episode; spectacle 5 — a god eating a trench of porridge on camera is exactly the show); myth_celtic_dagda_harp_recovery; myth_celtic_morrigan_ford_union (shared with the Morrígan).

## Manannán mac Lir

**Primary texts:** Immram Brain / The Voyage of Bran (Meyer tr. 1895, PD): Manannán rides his chariot across the sea, which is to him a flowering plain — the definitive mist-and-otherworld image. Serglige Con Culainn / The Sick-Bed of Cú Chulainn (Leahy, Heroic Romances of Ireland 1905, PD): he takes back his wife Fand and shakes his cloak of forgetfulness between her and Cú Chulainn so they may never meet again. The Crane Bag (Duanaire Finn, ed. MacNeill 1908): made from the skin of Aoife transformed into a crane; holds his treasures; passes through keepers to Cumhall and finally Fionn — verified via Duanaire Finn coverage and storyarchaeology.com. Gear-loans are his signature: Fragarach, Aonbharr, Wave-Sweeper lent to Lugh (Oidheadh Chloinne Tuireann, Joyce PD). **Manx folk continuity (verified):** the 'Supposed True Chronicle of Man' (16th c.) and the Manx Traditionary Ballad record Manannan as first ruler of Man, taking as tax a bundle of green rushes each Midsummer Eve at South Barrule — the 'paying the rent' custom, revived today.

**Axis rationale:** generosity 4 (the great lender); strictness 3 (terms attach; everything returns); pettiness 2; wrath 2 (mist, not violence); fidelity 2 (aloof — comes, goes, and cuts ties cleanly with the cloak); risk_appetite 3.

**Myth leads:** myth_celtic_voyage_of_bran (world_myth-adjacent otherworld geography); myth_celtic_sickbed_cu_chulainn (heroic_epic; shared with Cú Chulainn); myth_celtic_crane_bag (heroic_epic; shared with Fionn); myth_celtic_manx_paying_the_rent (local_legend/folk_tale).

## Nuada Airgetlám

**Primary texts:** Cath Maige Tuired (Stokes, PD) §§8–11: loses his arm to Sreng at the First Battle of Mag Tuired; the blemish law bars a maimed king, so kingship passes to Bres; Dian Cécht and the wright Creidhne make him a working silver arm (later, in the physician's-sons episode, flesh regrows); §33ish: restored as king when Bres is deposed; at the Second Battle he falls to Balor. His sword from Findias is one of the Four Treasures — none escape it once drawn (LGE / CMT §5–6).

**Axis rationale:** generosity 3 (magnanimous — steps aside for the abler Lugh at the war council); strictness 4 (embodies the perfection-of-kingship law, and submits to it against his own interest); pettiness 1 (no grudge recorded, ever); wrath 2; fidelity 4 (holds to his people through deposition and returns); risk_appetite 4 (staked kingship on battle twice; died on the second — flagged forsaken_candidate in census, confirmed: he knows exactly what unmaking costs, which is what a Forsaken host sells).

**Myth leads:** myth_celtic_nuada_silver_arm (heroic_epic; reenactment hook writes itself in a per-limb-HP game); myth_celtic_second_battle_mag_tuired (shared).

## Bres

**Primary texts:** Cath Maige Tuired (Stokes, PD; also Gray 1982 as academic check; open.maricopa.edu carries a teaching edition — verified via search). §36–39: under Bres's kingship 'the knives of the Tuatha Dé were not greased by him and their breaths did not smell of ale'; the poet Coirpre, lodged in a dark hut with no fire and dry bread, speaks the FIRST SATIRE in Ireland — 'without food quickly on a dish...' — and from that hour Bres declines; blisters bloom on the face of the most beautiful of kings, and a blemished king cannot reign. Deposed, he raises the Fomorians against his own people. End of CMT (verified quotes via search): captured unprotected, he bargains for his life — the cows of Ireland always in milk: rejected; a harvest every quarter: rejected; finally spared for pure agronomy — 'on Tuesday their ploughing, on Tuesday their sowing, on Tuesday their reaping.' A god who talked his existence back from the table in four bids.

**Axis rationale:** generosity 1 (his stinginess is THE canonical example — hospitality-failure deposed him); strictness 5 (taxation and forced labor on the gods themselves); pettiness 4 (a war of wounded pride); wrath 3; fidelity 1 (changes sides to whoever holds his interests); risk_appetite 5 (wagered his life on successive offers and kept bidding — the purest all-in temperament in the batch).

**Myth leads:** myth_celtic_first_satire_of_bres (local_legend; satire-as-weapon is a Celtic signature mechanic); myth_celtic_bres_bargain_for_life (heroic_epic episode; the negotiation minigame source); myth_celtic_second_battle_mag_tuired (shared).

## Cernunnos

**Attestation (this is the whole corpus):** one damaged inscribed name '[C]ernunnos' above an antlered, torc-hung figure on the Pillar of the Boatmen (Paris, 1st c. CE, Musée de Cluny); the antlered, cross-legged figure holding torc and ram-horned serpent on the Gundestrup Cauldron (conventional identification); the Reims relief with coin/grain sack; Val Camonica rock art. **No narrative myth survives — none.** The census one-liner said it and verification agrees (Wikipedia-index on Cernunnos/Pillar of the Boatmen, checked against museum-level descriptions). Modern afterlife: Wicca's Horned God is widely called Cernunnos and Celtic Reconstructionists venerate him by name (verified via Celtic-neopaganism overviews) — hence influence 3 with recognition 4 (SMITE playable since 2018; the horned-god silhouette is everywhere).

**Axis rationale — flagged LOW CONFIDENCE, iconography-derived, no narrative basis:** all six axes sit near median by necessity: generosity 3 (coins and grain pour from his imagery), strictness 3, pettiness 1 and wrath 2 (nothing hostile attested; he sits, calm, beasts at peace around him), fidelity 3, risk_appetite 2 (the stillest figure in Gaulish art). The dossier records these as a defensible default from attested iconography, NOT myth-derived numbers; patron_capable is FALSE because the ≥1 myth_ref bar cannot honestly be met. Wave 4 outlier-check should treat his axes as provisional.

**Myth leads:** none — a myth that cannot be sourced does not exist (spec §7). His game value is the gap itself.

## Cú Chulainn

**Primary texts:** Táin Bó Cúailnge (Dunn 1914, PD): single-handed defense of Ulster at the ford; the ríastrad warp-spasm; the Morrígan episodes; Lugh's paternal healing; the duel of Ferdiad killed by the Gáe Bulg — the barbed spear cast with the foot that opens into thirty barbs and must be cut out of the body. Aided Con Culainn / Cuchulainn's Death (Stokes tr., Revue Celtique 3, 1877, PD): trapped between geasa — never eat dog-flesh vs never refuse hospitality — by three crones roasting a hound; mortally wounded, he binds himself to the standing stone to die on his feet, and no enemy dares approach until the scald-crow settles on his shoulder. Serglige Con Culainn (Leahy 1905, PD): the Fand affair and the cloak of forgetfulness.

**Axis rationale:** generosity 3; strictness 4 (lives and dies by geasa); pettiness 2; wrath 5 (the ríastrad is wrath as body-horror); fidelity 5 (dies rather than abandon post or word); risk_appetite 5 (took the army alone at seventeen; took the field dying).

**Myth leads:** myth_celtic_tain_bo_cuailnge (heroic_epic umbrella); myth_celtic_morrigan_offer_and_ford_attacks; myth_celtic_lugh_heals_cu_chulainn; myth_celtic_death_of_cu_chulainn (heroic_epic; spectacle 5 — dying standing so the enemy stays afraid is peak reality-TV mythology); myth_celtic_sickbed_cu_chulainn.

## Fionn mac Cumhaill

**Primary texts:** Macgnímartha Finn / The Boyish Exploits of Finn (Meyer tr., PD-era): set to cook the Salmon of Knowledge for the poet Finnéces, the boy burns his thumb, sucks it, and takes the wisdom meant for his master — thereafter the thumb of knowledge on demand. Tóraigheacht Dhiarmada agus Ghráinne / The Pursuit of Diarmuid and Gráinne (Joyce, Old Celtic Romances 1879, PD): the aged Fionn, whose cupped hands of well-water can heal, twice lets the water slip through his fingers as the gored Diarmuid dies. Duanaire Finn: inherits the crane bag (verified above, §Manannán). Giant's Causeway folk cycle keeps 'Finn McCool' in global circulation.

**Axis rationale:** generosity 4 (proverbial open hand in the Fenian ballads); strictness 3; pettiness 4 (the water through the fingers — twice); wrath 3; fidelity 3 (a jealous old age); risk_appetite 3.

**Myth leads:** myth_celtic_salmon_of_knowledge (heroic_epic; reenactment: taste-first cooking event); myth_celtic_pursuit_diarmuid_grainne (heroic_epic); myth_celtic_crane_bag (shared).

## Lleu Llaw Gyffes

**Primary texts:** The Fourth Branch of the Mabinogi, Math fab Mathonwy (Guest tr., PD; Davies 2007 as academic check): Arianrhod swears three destinies on him — no name, no arms, no human wife — each undone by Gwydion's tricks (he is named for one skillful cast at a wren; armed via a conjured invasion; wed to Blodeuwedd, conjured from oak, broom, and meadowsweet). Blodeuwedd and Gronw Pebr contrive the impossible death: neither indoors nor outdoors, neither on foot nor horseback, by a spear forged only during Sunday mass for a year — bathing on a riverbank under a thatched frame, one foot on a cauldron's edge and one on a goat's back, he is struck, becomes an eagle, and rots in an oak until Gwydion finds him and sings him back to manhood with three englynion. He repays Gronw exactly: a spear-cast through a stone Gronw is allowed to hold as shield (Llech Ronw).

**Axis rationale:** generosity 3; strictness 4 (his whole story is conditions, oaths, and exact repayment); pettiness 2 (grants Gronw the stone — then kills him through it: justice, not spite); wrath 3; fidelity 4; risk_appetite 4 (naively demonstrates his own death-pose on request — catastrophic trust in stated rules).

**Myth leads:** myth_celtic_fourth_branch_mabinogi (heroic_epic; two reenactment hooks — assemble-the-loophole and resurrection-by-verses).

## Balor

**Primary texts:** Cath Maige Tuired (Stokes, PD): the destroying eye was envenomed when fumes from his father's druids' brew touched it; it is opened only in battle, the lid hauled up by a polished handle by four men, and an army that looks on it cannot resist warriors; Lugh's sling-stone carries the eye through his head so its gaze falls on his own Fomorian host; the prophecy that his grandson would kill him is fulfilled. Folk versions (Tory Island cycle, 19th-c. collections) add the imprisoned daughter Eithne — indexed for Wave 3 only if a printed PD source is pinned down.

**Axis rationale:** generosity 1; strictness 4; pettiness 3; wrath 5 (the eye is annihilation as anatomy); fidelity 2 (imprisons his own daughter against prophecy); risk_appetite 2 (a tyrant who tried to cheat fate rather than bet against it — he is the table, not the gambler).

**Myth leads:** myth_celtic_second_battle_mag_tuired (his death is its climax).

## Twrch Trwyth

**Primary texts:** Culhwch and Olwen (Guest tr., PD; verified via EBSCO Research Starters and Mabinogion editions): a king transformed into a giant boar for his sins, carrying comb, razor, and shears between his ears — the implements Ysbaddaden demands for his grooming before the wedding; Arthur's whole warband hunts him from Ireland across Wales; razor and shears are snatched at the Severn mouth, the comb only in Cornwall; he is driven into the sea, never slain. His seven piglets die defending him.

**Axis rationale:** generosity 1; strictness 3; pettiness 4 (lays waste countryside in answer to being hunted); wrath 5; fidelity 4 (his piglets' loyalty is mirrored — he stands over their bodies); risk_appetite 3.

**Myth leads:** myth_celtic_hunt_twrch_trwyth (heroic_epic; grade-A objective-boss reenactment: steal three items from a boss that cannot be killed).

## Leprechaun

**Primary texts:** early attestation as luchorpán in the Saga of Fergus mac Léti (medieval, ed. Binchy); the modern figure via T. Crofton Croker, Fairy Legends and Traditions of the South of Ireland (1825, PD — Gutenberg #39752, verified): solitary fairy shoemaker, buried crock of gold, escape by misdirection — 'The Field of Boliauns' ends with every ragwort in the field tied with an identical red garter. Croker's 'cluricaune' is the regional variant. Older dress is RED; the green suit is a modern (largely 20th-c. American) convention — an honest art-direction deep cut.

**Axis rationale:** generosity 1 (hoards); strictness 5 (contracts honored to the letter, never the spirit); pettiness 4; wrath 2; fidelity 2 (no deal binds him longer than your attention span); risk_appetite 4 (will always take the wager — he has already engineered the out).

**Myth leads:** myth_celtic_field_of_boliauns (folk_tale; reenactment: the look-away wager).

## Banshee

**Primary texts:** T. Crofton Croker, Fairy Legends and Traditions of the South of Ireland (1825, PD — verified via Gutenberg #39752): banshee narratives including the Bunworth Banshee (Rev. Charles Bunworth, Co. Cork, 1770) and the MacCarthy banshee material; the bean sí keens outside the house before a death in the old families; regional overlap with the badhbh (hooded-crow death woman). The silver comb lure is a widespread attached folk motif (indexed; Wave 3 should pin a specific printed variant before writing the comb myth as its own record).

**Axis rationale:** generosity 2 (the keen is, functionally, a warning service); strictness 3; pettiness 2; wrath 1 (she harms no one — she only announces); fidelity 5 (bound to particular lineages for generations — the highest fidelity in the batch); risk_appetite 1.

**Myth leads:** myth_celtic_bunworth_banshee (folk_tale, documented 1825; the elimination-herald mechanic source).
